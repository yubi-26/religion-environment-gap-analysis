# Conclusion

## Summary of Findings

This study examined the relationship between religious composition and carbon emissions using cross-national data from 181 countries. Addressing the endogeneity of religious composition through an instrumental variables strategy based on historical geographic diffusion (latitude and distance from Mecca), we find **no evidence** that Muslim population share has a causal effect on CO₂ emissions per capita.

Our results consistently show that:

1. **Economic development** (GDP per capita) is the primary driver of carbon emissions
2. **Religious identity** has no statistically significant effect once GDP is controlled
3. **Gender inequality** does not mediate the relationship between religion and environment

These findings challenge culturally deterministic narratives that attribute environmental outcomes to religious identity.

## Policy Implications

1. **Environmental policy should focus on economic and institutional reforms**, not cultural or religious change
2. **The narrative linking Islam to environmental degradation is empirically unsupported** and should be critically examined
3. **Developing countries face environmental challenges due to economic structure**, not religious composition

## Limitations and Future Research

While this study provides robust evidence, several limitations should be acknowledged:

1. **Cross-national data** may mask within-country variation
2. **CO₂ emissions** are one of many environmental outcomes
3. **Instrumental variables** rely on the exclusion restriction assumption

Future research could extend this analysis by:

- Examining other environmental outcomes (forest cover, air quality, water stress)
- Exploring alternative channels such as institutional quality and governance
- Using sub-national data within countries
- Incorporating additional instruments (colonial history, missionary presence)

## Conclusion

The central message of this research is clear: **economic structure, not religious identity, is the fundamental determinant of national carbon emissions**. This finding has important implications for how we understand the relationship between culture, development, and environmental outcomes.
